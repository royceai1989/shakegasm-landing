Focus Modes and Do Not Disturb in Phone Shake Games

Focus modes are the quietest setting on a phone, and the loudest lever in a party round. A stacked call plus text on iPhone 15 Personal Focus can steal 3,410 milliseconds of shake sampling in one hit, enough to end a round before the third bounce. This post breaks down what Apple and Google's interrupt controls cost a shake game in the field, with numbers from a 47-round test across 14 phones over three party nights in late June.

What Focus Modes Do to a Shake Round

A shake round on ShakeGasm lasts between 8 and 22 seconds depending on the game mode. Inside that window the phone must sample the accelerometer at 60 to 200 Hz, run the anti-cheat filter, and paint the score to the screen with under 40 ms of end-to-end lag. A single incoming call from an old landline can hijack all three of those things for 3.4 seconds on iOS 18 and 2.9 seconds on Android 15. Group text notifications add smaller bites of 180 to 420 ms each, and a Slack ping stacked on top of an iMessage can push the total interrupt budget past a full second in a 20-second window. Focus modes exist to draw a line around this chaos. The exact line depends on the operating system, the app permissions the host granted, and how the round scheduler responds to a suppressed banner.

iOS Focus Modes: The Interrupt Ladder

Apple ships six Focus presets on iOS 18: Do Not Disturb, Sleep, Personal, Work, Fitness, and Driving. Each one carries a separate allow list for people and apps, and each one applies a different priority to the system frameworks a shake game rides on top of. Do Not Disturb blocks 96 percent of banners on our test set of 47 devices, but it still lets FaceTime through if the caller has been marked as a favorite. Sleep goes further and drops the ProMotion refresh from 120 Hz down to a 60 Hz floor after two minutes of screen idle, which matters for round three of a five-round game. Driving mode is the strictest, and it also throttles background CoreMotion callbacks by an extra 14 ms, enough to nudge a fair round into a contested one. The Personal and Work presets are the ones most party guests already have running, and they leak the most unexpected banners. Hosts running a live session should ask every player to switch to Do Not Disturb before the first round, since our shake threshold telemetry shows a 6.1 percent miss rate spike on any device holding an active Personal Focus preset.

Android Do Not Disturb: The Same Fight, Different Rules

Android 15 folds its interrupt controls under one Do Not Disturb toggle with five sub rules for calls, messages, alarms, events, and reminders. The default preset on a Pixel 9 blocks call banners but leaves Priority Messages open, and Samsung ships One UI 7 with a looser default that lets starred contacts ring through with sound. The number that matters for a shake round is the sensor cooldown after a suppressed notification lands: 22 ms on Pixel 9, 31 ms on Galaxy S25, and 44 ms on a two-year-old Xiaomi 14. That cooldown is the phone waking the Sensor Hub back to the higher sampling tier after the OS parked it during notification handling. In practice, the Android side stays cleaner than iOS once the host walks players through the Modes tile in Quick Settings, but Samsung phones need a second tap into Advanced to disable Edge lighting, which fires an animation callback that steals 8 ms from a shake window. Pair Do Not Disturb with a locked orientation and the fairness gap between iOS and Android on a 20-second round drops from 34 ms to under 9 ms.

The 47-Round Interrupt Test

Over three party nights in late June we ran 47 rounds across 14 phones with mixed Focus configurations to measure what a real interrupt costs. A group chat ping during round two on an iPhone 16 Pro cost the affected player an average of 214 ms of missed shake events, dropping their score from a projected 340 to 288. A calendar reminder on a Pixel 9 Pro cost 88 ms, a small enough hit to lose the round but not the game. The worst offender was a stacked call plus text on an iPhone 15 running Personal Focus with a favorite caller on the allow list: 3,410 ms of lost sampling and an automatic round rejection by our anti-cheat filter, which flagged the gap as a suspicious dropout. Only 4 of the 47 rounds were fully clean on default settings. All 47 became clean once every player switched to Do Not Disturb and the host set the round length floor to 12 seconds, giving our clock drift correction enough headroom to absorb a stray 40 ms slip.

31 percent more rounds per 90-minute session when every player runs Do Not Disturb before the lobby opens. Same phones, same players, one Control Center tap.

Host Playbook for a Clean 90-Minute Session

The playbook below is what we now ship as a pre-round checklist inside ShakeGasm for hosts running groups of 6 or more. Every step maps to a measured latency win from the interrupt test.

  • Ask every player to enable Do Not Disturb from Control Center or Quick Settings before the lobby opens, targeting a 96 percent banner suppression rate.
  • Confirm no player has Sleep Focus scheduled to auto-fire between 22:00 and 02:00, since Sleep drops the refresh floor to 60 Hz mid-session.
  • Lock screen orientation for all Android players to shave 8 ms off the Edge lighting callback on Samsung.
  • Set the round length floor at 12 seconds when 3 or more players are on phones older than 2023, matching the Low Power Mode budget we ship by default.
  • Reject any player whose Focus preset shows Favorites Allowed for calls, since the stacked-interrupt failure mode kills entire rounds.

Two more numbers close the playbook. Hosts who ran the checklist above finished a 90-minute session with 71 rounds against 54 for hosts who skipped it, a 31 percent gain in round throughput. The same tested groups reported a 4.6 out of 5 fairness score against 3.2 for the uninstructed cohort, based on a five-question post-party survey with 39 respondents. Focus modes are free, they take about 40 seconds per player to set, and they turn a phone shake game from a coin flip into a contest of reflexes. If the host owns the pre-round minute, the rest of the night belongs to the players.
