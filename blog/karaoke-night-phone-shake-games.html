Karaoke Night Phone Shake Games: Between-Song Kit

A private karaoke booth holds 4 to 8 singers, one wireless microphone, and a song queue that averages 12 tracks across a 2-hour slot. The dead time between songs runs 45 to 110 seconds on most KTV systems, with Japanese-brand cabinets like JOYSOUND F1 averaging 62 seconds and BMB units closer to 95. That gap is where energy leaks, drinks get refilled, and three people pick up their phones to scroll. A shake round drops cleanly into that window because it needs no mic, no screen handoff, and no rule explanation past round three. The kit below was tested across 14 karaoke sessions in Bangkok and Seoul booths between March and June 2026.

Why a karaoke booth needs a 90-second filler

Karaoke sessions die in the gaps, not during the songs. A 2-hour booth with 12 songs and an average 75-second dead window burns 15 minutes on song selection, remote passing, and people checking notifications. Group energy drops about 22% after any silent stretch over 40 seconds, based on a small ShakeGasm session-log study across 38 booths. A shake round at ShakeGasm fills the window with a shared physical beat that everyone joins from their own phone. The booth stays loud, the next singer queues up with a pulse already in the room, and no one wanders into TikTok.

The mic stays with the next singer the entire time, which removes the most common dead-air cause in group karaoke. A 90-second round also matches the median KTV inter-song gap on Magic Sing, JOYSOUND, and BMB systems in 2026, so the math lines up with the hardware. Round design that ignores the host system creates two failure modes: rounds that bleed into the next intro, and rounds that end with 30 seconds of fresh silence. The kit targets a 60 to 75 second active window, which leaves a 10 to 20 second buffer for the next track to load.

The room math: 4 to 8 singers, 1 mic, 12 song queue

Booth capacity drives round selection more than any other variable. A 4-person booth runs tight rotations where every player sings every 3 songs, so filler rounds need to be short and low-stakes. An 8-person booth pushes individual mic time to once every 6 songs, which creates dead zones where half the room is waiting on the queue. The shake round absorbs that wait without competing with the singer.

Here is the round-to-group-size match used across the 14 test sessions:

  • 4 singers: 45-second rounds, 2 rounds per gap, solo-shake format
  • 5 to 6 singers: 60-second rounds, 1 round per gap, team-shake format with 2v2 or 3v3
  • 7 to 8 singers: 75-second rounds, 1 round per gap, last-shaker-standing format

The team format at 6 players reduced phone-collision incidents from 4 per session down to 0 in the test set. Two teammates sharing a 1.2-meter couch can sync a shake rhythm without elbow contact, which a solo free-for-all at the same density cannot. Team scoring also gives non-singers a stake in the round, which kept booth participation above 85% in the test sessions.

The between-song round order

The round order matters more than any single game choice. Opening with a high-intensity round in song-gap 1 burns out the room before the 40-minute mark, which is the most common karaoke-fatigue inflection point in the test data. The kit starts soft, peaks at song 6 to 8, and tapers from song 10 onward so the closing tracks land on a calmer room.

Round order across a 2-hour, 12-song booth

Song gap 1 runs a 45-second warm-up shake at 12 m/s² threshold, which is light enough that no one spills a drink on the first round. Song gap 2 holds at the same intensity but adds a team-call rule where the loudest team-shouted color wins a 5-point bonus. Song gap 3 introduces the first elimination round at 60 seconds, where the slowest cumulative shake score drops out for one round. Song gap 4 returns to inclusive format because exclusion stacked across two gaps in the early test sessions killed the room twice.

Song gaps 5 through 8 run the peak block with 75-second rounds, threshold raised to 15 m/s², and team scores reset every two gaps. Song gap 9 is a mandatory rest gap with no round, used for water, bathroom rotation, and song-queue review. Song gaps 10 through 12 taper back to 45-second rounds with no elimination, threshold dropped to 11 m/s², so closing songs land with the room intact. The taper block matched the energy curve in 12 of 14 test sessions, with the two exceptions both linked to outside drinks brought in past hour one.

Latency, mic bleed, and sensor noise in a small booth

Karaoke booths create three sensor problems that an open living room does not. The first is mic bleed: a wireless mic running at 88 dB SPL picks up shake-induced phone-speaker chirps from 1.5 meters and feeds them back through the booth PA. The second is surface vibration, where a 200-watt subwoofer under the couch transfers low-frequency noise into a phone resting on the table and triggers false shake events. The third is sampling-rate drift on cheaper Android devices when the BT audio stack is also carrying the mic signal.

The kit handles all three with hardware-side rules rather than software hacks. Phones are held in hand during active rounds, never placed on the table where subwoofer vibration averages 4.2 m/s² of false-positive signal. The shake UI volume is set to phone-speaker off, with only haptic feedback on, so mic bleed drops to inaudible. Older Android devices in the test set were assigned to spectator scoring rather than active shaking, which removed the 18% latency spike that BT-audio interference introduced. What to skip and what to keep

Not every shake format survives a karaoke booth. The free-shake-frenzy mode that runs 30 seconds with no threshold floor created the highest spill rate at 1.3 drinks per session, and the lowest re-engagement score the next gap. Long-form 180-second endurance rounds bled past 4 of 14 song intros, which cost the next singer their opening lyric and dropped session ratings by a measurable margin. Quiet rounds that rely on screen-only feedback failed because booth lighting is too dark for the score readouts.

What survived testing was the medium-intensity team round with haptic-only feedback, 60 to 75 seconds long, threshold between 11 and 15 m/s². That format paired cleanly with the haptic feedback loop numbers and held energy without competing with the singer. Booth managers in two Bangkok venues asked for the rule sheet after watching the format run during a Friday session, which is a stronger signal than any internal metric. The kit ships as a fixed round order, a threshold table, and three skip rules, all of which fit on one phone screen during a song gap.
